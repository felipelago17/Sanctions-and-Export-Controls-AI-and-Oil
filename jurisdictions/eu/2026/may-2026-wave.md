- **Jurisdiction / Authority:** EU — High Representative of the Union for Foreign Affairs and Security Policy (speaking on behalf of the EU)
- **Legal instrument / basis:** EU restrictive measures on Iran — Council Regulation (EU) No 359/2011 (human rights violations); Council Regulation (EU) No 267/2012 (non-proliferation / nuclear programme); related Council Decisions. Third-country alignment operates through political declarations by partner states adopting equivalent measures at the national level.
- **Targets:** This is an alignment statement — not itself a designation action. The named third countries confirm they are aligning their own national restrictive measures with existing EU Iran sanctions lists. Countries that routinely align include EU candidate states (Western Balkans: North Macedonia, Montenegro, Albania, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Kosovo), Eastern Partnership members (Moldova, Georgia), and others.
- **Sector / theme:** Iran — human rights (domestic oppression); nuclear non-proliferation; regional destabilisation; third-country multilateral alignment
- **Summary:** The EU High Representative confirmed that a group of partner countries have aligned their national measures with EU restrictive measures directed against persons and entities connected to the situation in Iran. Alignment statements extend the effective geographic reach of EU sanctions beyond EU member states without requiring the aligned countries to enact independent legal frameworks. This statement is contemporaneous with Australia's DFAT autonomous sanctions targeting Iran's campaign of oppression and destabilisation (AU-2026-001) — together these constitute a **coordinated multi-jurisdiction Iran pressure campaign** in May 2026. Compliance practitioners should note that the same individuals/entities on EU Iran lists will now also be caught by the national measures of the aligned countries, increasing the extraterritorial compliance burden for multinationals.

- **Legal instrument / basis:** EU–Syria Association/Cooperation Agreement; Council Decision and/or Regulation suspending or reinstating the agreement. Context: EU Syria sanctions under Council Regulation (EU) No 36/2012 (as amended through successive restrictive measures packages targeting the Assad regime).
- **Targets:** N/A — this is a sanctions-easing / normalisation decision, not a new designation action.
- **Sector / theme:** Syria — post-conflict normalisation; sanctions relief; re-engagement compliance; contrast with simultaneous tightening in this wave
- **Summary:** The EU Council restored full application of the EU–Syria Cooperation Agreement, a significant step in normalising economic and political engagement with post-Assad Syria. Following the fall of the Assad regime in December 2024, the EU began a phased approach to easing Syria sanctions: initially suspending specific sectoral restrictions to enable humanitarian access and reconstruction, and progressively lifting suspensions as the new Syrian authorities consolidate governance. Restoring the Cooperation Agreement unlocks trade preferences, development financing, and institutional engagement. **Compliance note for EU persons and entities re-engaging with Syria:** While this decision signals normalisation, residual Syria sanctions (designations of Assad-era officials and entities, arms embargo provisions) likely remain in force — legal clearance is required before resuming previously prohibited activities. A bespoke sanctions review is advisable before any Syrian counterparty is onboarded.

### Easing vs. Tightening — Contrast with Simultaneous Actions

On the same date (11 May 2026), the EU also:
- Designated 16 individuals and 7 entities for the forced transfer of Ukrainian children (EU-2026-001) — **tightening**
- Issued an Iran alignment statement (EU-2026-002) — **tightening / multilateral pressure**

The Syria decision demonstrates that the EU uses its sanctions toolkit flexibly — easing where normalisation is the policy goal while simultaneously tightening against Russia and Iran. For compliance teams, this means Syria review processes must distinguish between what remains prohibited and what has been unlocked.
