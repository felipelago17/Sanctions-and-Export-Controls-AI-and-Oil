# Encryption Controls, Open Source & EAR Scope

Encryption items have their own licensing and reporting framework under EAR Part 742.15 and Supplement No. 3. Many AI and software products incorporate encryption, making these rules directly relevant. Open-source software raises distinct questions about EAR applicability.

---

## eCFR — 15 C.F.R. § 742.15 (Encryption Controls)

**URL:** <https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-15/subtitle-B/chapter-VII/subchapter-C/part-742/section-742.15>

The primary regulatory text governing encryption export controls under the EAR. Covers ECCN 5E002 (encryption technology), the mass-market encryption exception, annual self-classification reporting requirements (via the Encryption Registration portal), and the review process for non-US government end-users.

---

## BIS — Encryption Items Not Subject to EAR

**URL:** <https://media.bis.gov/learn-support/encryption-controls/encryption-items-not-subject-to-ear>

Official BIS guidance identifying categories of encryption products that fall outside EAR jurisdiction entirely: publicly available software with no US-person involvement, certain open-source cryptographic libraries, and products subject only to EAR99 classification. Critical for open-source AI projects that incorporate cryptographic modules.

---

## Encryption Authority — US Export Controls on Encryption

**URL:** <https://encryptionauthority.com/us-export-controls-on-encryption>

Practitioner-oriented overview of the full encryption control framework: classification under ECCN 5x002, the Section 740.17 licence exceptions (ENC), mass-market eligibility criteria, and the annual reporting obligation. Useful companion to the eCFR text.

---

## Baker McKenzie — BIS Updates Reporting Requirements for Mass-Market Encryption & Publicly Available Software

**URL:** <https://sanctionsnews.bakermckenzie.com/bis-updates-reporting-requirements-relating-to-mass-market-encryption-items-and-publicly-available-software-and-also-updates-certain-classifications/>

Analysis of BIS amendments to annual self-classification reporting for mass-market encryption products. Covers how changes affect software-as-a-service (SaaS) providers and cloud platforms that deliver encryption functionality, and the interaction with the "publicly available" exclusion for open-source software.
